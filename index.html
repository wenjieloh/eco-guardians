THE BASICS

What Is an Invasive Plant?

🌍

Not From Here

Invasive plants are species introduced — accidentally or on purpose — outside their native range. In Singapore, many arrived via trade ships or were planted as ornamentals.

⚡

Spreads Uncontrollably

Without natural predators or diseases from their home country, they spread unchecked — sometimes doubling coverage in weeks.

💀

Smothers Native Life

They steal sunlight, water, and nutrients from native plants — cascading into habitat loss for Singapore's wildlife.

🔥

Incredibly Hard to Eradicate

Once established, invasive plants cost enormous time, money, and effort to remove — prevention and early action are everything.

SINGAPORE'S STORY

Why Singapore Is Especially Vulnerable

As one of the world's busiest ports and a major aviation hub, Singapore is a gateway through which invasive species constantly enter — hidden in cargo, soil, produce, and even tourists' luggage.


Our tropical climate — warm, wet, and year-round growing season — is ideal for invasive plants to establish and spread rapidly. Singapore's fragmented green spaces make it even harder for native ecosystems to recover once invaded.


NParks' City in Nature initiative aims to restore and protect Singapore's biodiversity. But managing invasives is one of its biggest challenges. That's where you come in.

What Makes a Plant "Invasive"?

An invasive plant is a species that is non-native to an ecosystem and whose introduction causes — or is likely to cause — harm to the environment, economy, or human health.


Not every foreign plant is invasive. A plant becomes truly invasive when it:

  • Establishes and reproduces successfully in a new environment
  • Spreads rapidly without human help
  • Outcompetes native species for sunlight, water, and nutrients
  • Disrupts native ecosystems and the animals that depend on them

In Singapore, invasive plants arrived via centuries of global trade, colonial-era ornamental planting, and the aquarium and gardening trades. Today, over 400 non-native plant species have been recorded here — many of them causing real damage.

Native vs Invasive — Singapore Example

🌸 Tembusu Tree (Native)
  • Evolved in local conditions
  • Feeds native insects & birds
  • Supports 50+ animal species
  • Appears on Singapore $5 note
  • Grows in natural balance
🌿 Zanzibar Yam (Invasive)
  • Introduced from Africa/Asia
  • No local natural enemies
  • Smothers trees and shrubs
  • Spreads via bulbils in soil
  • Takes over rapidly
🧬

The "Enemy Release" Effect

In Africa, the Zanzibar Yam is kept in check by insects, fungi, and animals that evolved alongside it. In Singapore, none of these natural controls exist — so it grows explosively.

🚢

Singapore's Port Problem

As the world's second-busiest port, Singapore receives cargo from 600+ ports globally. Seeds and plant fragments arrive constantly — hidden in soil, packaging, and fresh produce.

🌡️

Climate Change Makes It Worse

Rising temperatures and changing rainfall patterns in Singapore create even more favourable conditions for tropical invasives to establish and spread year-round.

How Do Invasive Plants Spread in Singapore?

Singapore's unique geography — island nation, major port, dense urban-nature mix — creates specific pathways for invasive plants to spread:

🚢

Shipping & Trade

Seeds hidden in soil or cargo are the biggest entry point. Singapore's port handles over 37 million containers per year — each one a potential vector.

🌊

Waterways & Drains

Singapore's canal network connects nature reserves to the coast. Plants like Water Hyacinth spread rapidly through our drainage systems into reservoirs and parks.

🐦

Birds & Wildlife

Birds eat berries of invasive plants like Clidemia hirta and Lantana and deposit seeds throughout our nature reserves — sometimes covering kilometres in a single day.

🏡

Irresponsible Gardening

Aquarium plants dumped into canals and ornamental plants dumped at forest edges are major problems in Singapore. Never release garden plants into the wild!

🏗️

Construction & Soil Movement

Singapore's constant construction moves soil — and the seeds and rhizomes within it — to new locations. Invasives thrive in disturbed soil at construction edges.

🌱

Underground Bulbils & Rhizomes

The Zanzibar Yam produces bulbils (small bulb-like structures) that drop from vines and remain dormant in soil for years, sprouting when conditions are right.

Singapore's Most Threatened Habitats

Singapore has some of South-East Asia's most precious and fragmented ecosystems — all under threat from invasive plants:

🌳

Bukit Timah Forest

One of the world's most biodiverse urban forests. Mile-a-Minute and Zanzibar Yam are aggressively invading its edges, threatening endemic species found nowhere else on Earth.

⚠️ Mile-a-Minute, Zanzibar Yam, Clidemia hirta
🌊

Reservoirs & Waterways

Singapore's reservoirs and canals supply our drinking water. Water Hyacinth mats reduce water quality, block sunlight, and clog pumping systems.

⚠️ Water Hyacinth, Water Lettuce, Salvinia
🌿

Mangroves (Sungei Buloh)

Singapore's mangroves are critical nurseries for fish and shrimp, and resting spots for migratory birds. Invasive grasses and vines are fragmenting these habitats.

⚠️ Casuarina, Para Grass
🏝️

Pulau Ubin

Singapore's last kampong island is a biodiversity refuge. But invasive plants are filling gaps left by abandoned farmland — outcompeting returning native species.

⚠️ Siam Weed, Lantana, Senduduk Bulu
🌺

Grasslands & Wastelands

Open areas between urban and forested zones are invasion hotspots. Invasive grasses establish here first, then creep into adjacent forests.

⚠️ Siam Weed, Cogon Grass, Albizia
🦅

Southern Ridges

This green corridor connecting Labrador to Mount Faber is under constant invasion pressure from urban-adapted invasive plants spreading from surrounding housing estates.

⚠️ Senduduk Bulu, Heliconia, Albizia

The Damage They Cause in Singapore 💀

The impact of invasive plants goes far beyond just looking untidy. Here's what's really at stake for Singapore:

🌿 Ecological Damage

  • Biodiversity collapse — Invasive plants replace diverse native communities with single-species monocultures
  • Wildlife food web disruption — Native insects, birds, and mammals depend on specific native plants that invasives displace
  • Soil chemistry changes — Some invasives alter soil pH and nutrient levels, preventing native plants from re-establishing
  • Light competition — Fast-growing vines like Mile-a-Minute grow over forest canopy, starving trees of sunlight until they die
  • Seed bank disruption — Invasive bulbils and seeds persist in soil for years, making regeneration of native species nearly impossible

💰 Economic & Infrastructure Damage

  • Water supply costs — Water Hyacinth mats require expensive mechanical and chemical treatment in reservoirs
  • PUB drainage blockage — Aquatic weeds block Singapore's canal systems, requiring costly maintenance
  • NParks removal budgets — Singapore spends tens of millions annually managing invasive plants in parks and reserves
  • Tourism impact — Degraded green spaces reduce the appeal of Singapore's nature parks, which attract significant eco-tourism

🏥 Public Health Concerns

  • Dengue risk — Dense invasive plant mats create still-water habitats perfect for Aedes mosquito breeding
  • Toxic plants — Some invasive species in Singapore are toxic to children and pets if touched or ingested
  • Allergens — Invasive flowering plants can worsen respiratory conditions for sensitive individuals
  • Water quality — Invasive aquatic plants reduce dissolved oxygen, enabling bacteria and toxin-producing algae to bloom

🔦 Spotlight: What Mile-a-Minute Does to Bukit Timah

Mikania micrantha (Mile-a-Minute) is Singapore's most problematic invasive plant. Growing up to 8cm per day, it drapes itself over shrubs and trees like a thick green blanket, cutting off all light. Trees weaken and collapse. The seedbank becomes dominated by Mikania seeds. Native undergrowth vanishes entirely. Recovering a heavily invaded patch can take 10–20 years of active management — or it may never recover at all.

How Singapore Fights Back 🛡️

Singapore uses several integrated approaches to manage invasive plants — combining government action with community involvement:

✋

Manual Removal

NParks Standard

NParks staff and volunteers physically uproot invasive plants. Effective for small patches. Must remove all roots — even small fragments of Zanzibar Yam can regrow.

🧪

Targeted Herbicides

Specialist Use Only

Foliar sprays or stem injection with herbicides like glyphosate. Used carefully to minimise impact on native plants. Not applied near waterways without special protocols.

🌱

Native Replanting

Critical Follow-Up

After invasive removal, NParks replants native species to close the gap before invasives return. This is why volunteer planting events are so important!

🔬

Research & Monitoring

NUS + NParks

NUS researchers monitor invasive spread using GPS mapping and citizen science data from iNaturalist. Early detection data saves enormous costs in management.

📋

Biosecurity at Borders

AVA Regulated

Singapore's Agriculture, Food & Veterinary Authority (AVA) maintains a list of prohibited plants. All plant imports must be declared and inspected at borders.

🏫

Education & Community

Most Sustainable!

Long-term success depends on a public that understands invasive plants. School programmes, community events, and citizen science are Singapore's most powerful tools.

💡

Singapore-specific tip: If you see Water Hyacinth in a canal or reservoir, report it to PUB's 24-hour hotline (1800-284-6600) or NParks' feedback portal. Early reports save huge management costs!

Eight invasive plants causing real damage across Singapore. Click any card for the full profile.

EXTREME

Zanzibar Yam

Dioscorea bulbifera

"The Bulb Bomber"

📍 Africa & Asia🌍 Found: Island-wide📏 Up to 5m long

🏞️ Habitat in Singapore

Forest edges, secondary forests, roadsides, and garden fences across Singapore. Particularly aggressive at Bukit Timah, Central Catchment, and Pulau Ubin. Thrives in Singapore's year-round warmth and humidity.

💀 How It Kills

The Zanzibar Yam twines aggressively around native shrubs and trees, growing several metres high. Its heart-shaped leaves create a dense canopy that blocks all sunlight — smothering whatever is underneath. It produces bulbils (small aerial tubers) that drop to the ground, spread via soil disturbance, and remain viable for years.

📡 How It Spreads in SG

Bulbils drop from the vine and are moved by water flow, soil disturbance during construction, and sometimes by animals. The yam also spreads through underground tubers. Even small fragments of tuber left in soil can regenerate a full plant.

🛡️ How NParks Removes It

  • Manual uprooting — must remove all bulbils from soil
  • Repeated cutting over multiple seasons to exhaust root reserves
  • Herbicide stem injection for established plants
  • Monitoring for regrowth for at least 2 years after removal
🤯

Wild fact: The Zanzibar Yam is edible in its native range (when properly prepared) — it was likely introduced to Singapore as a food crop during colonial times. Now it's one of our most problematic invasives.

EXTREME

Mile-a-Minute

Mikania micrantha

"Singapore's #1 Weed"

📍 C. & S. America🌍 Found: Island-wide📏 8cm growth/day

🏞️ Habitat in Singapore

Roadsides, forest edges, degraded forest, wasteland, and gaps in secondary vegetation across Singapore. The single most widespread and problematic invasive plant in Singapore according to NParks. Found in virtually every nature area.

💀 How It Kills

Mile-a-Minute earns its name — growing up to 8cm per day in Singapore's tropical climate. It climbs over and smothers all vegetation beneath it, forming a continuous canopy that blocks all light. Affected trees and shrubs die within months. It produces thousands of tiny wind-dispersed seeds continuously throughout the year.

📡 How It Spreads in SG

Wind dispersal of lightweight seeds is the primary mechanism. Seeds travel from construction sites, roadsides, and disturbed areas into adjacent forest. The plant also spreads vegetatively — stem fragments root easily in Singapore's moist soil.

🛡️ How NParks Removes It

  • Manual cutting and removal before seed set — top priority action
  • Repeated weekly cutting during the growing season
  • Herbicide spraying on established infestations
  • Competitive planting of fast-growing native species to shade it out
🤯

Wild fact: Mile-a-Minute was accidentally introduced to Singapore via contaminated grass seed in the 1960s. A single preventable import mistake created decades of ongoing management costs and irreversible forest damage.

HIGH

Water Hyacinth

Eichhornia crassipes

"The Waterway Choker"

📍 S. America🌍 Found: Canals, reservoirs📏 Doubles in 2 weeks

🏞️ Habitat in Singapore

Found in Singapore's reservoirs, canals, and slow-moving waterways. Common in Kranji Reservoir, Sungei Buloh, and various urban canals. Particularly problematic after heavy rains wash it between water bodies.

💀 How It Kills

Forms thick floating mats that block sunlight to submerged aquatic plants. Dissolved oxygen drops dramatically as decomposing plant material consumes oxygen — fish and other aquatic life suffocate. The mats also create ideal breeding grounds for Aedes mosquitoes, increasing dengue risk.

📡 How It Spreads in SG

Doubles its coverage in as little as two weeks through vegetative reproduction. Flood events wash mats through Singapore's canal network from reservoir to reservoir. Sometimes introduced when people dump aquarium plants into waterways.

🛡️ How PUB & NParks Remove It

  • Mechanical harvesting using specialist boats on larger water bodies
  • Manual removal using nets and rakes in canals
  • Biological control research ongoing
  • NEVER dump aquarium plants into Singapore's waterways
🤯

Wild fact: Water Hyacinth was introduced to Singapore as an ornamental pond plant in the early 20th century because of its beautiful purple flowers. It's now one of our most costly aquatic invasives.

HIGH

Senduduk Bulu

Clidemia hirta

"The Forest Floor Killer"

📍 Tropical Americas🌍 Found: Forest understorey🍇 8,000 seeds per plant

🏞️ Habitat in Singapore

A serious understorey invader in Singapore's secondary forests and nature reserves, including Bukit Timah and Central Catchment. Unlike many invasives, it tolerates shade — meaning it can invade intact forest, not just disturbed edges.

💀 How It Kills

Forms dense shrubby thickets in the forest understorey, preventing native seedlings from establishing. This breaks the regeneration cycle of native forest — once Senduduk Bulu takes over the understorey, the forest cannot replace its canopy trees naturally as they die.

📡 How It Spreads in SG

Each plant produces thousands of small berries eagerly eaten by birds — especially the invasive Javan Myna. The myna disperses seeds throughout Singapore's forest fragments. This bird-plant invasive partnership makes Senduduk Bulu especially hard to control.

🛡️ How NParks Removes It

  • Manual uprooting — most effective when plants are young
  • Cut-stump herbicide treatment for established shrubs
  • Long-term monitoring — seeds persist in soil for years
  • Replanting native understorey species after clearing
🤯

Wild fact: Senduduk Bulu's invasion success in Singapore is partly powered by the Javan Myna — itself an invasive species! One invasive helping another is called an "invasion meltdown" by scientists.

HIGH

Siam Weed

Chromolaena odorata

"The Sunlight Thief"

📍 C. & S. America🌍 Found: Wasteland, edges📏 3m tall in 1 season

🏞️ Habitat in Singapore

Roadsides, wasteland, forest edges, and abandoned farmland — especially prevalent on Pulau Ubin and along Singapore's rail corridor. Highly adaptable and drought-tolerant for a tropical species.

💀 How It Kills

Grows up to 3 metres tall in a single growing season, shading out all native plants below. Produces allelopathic chemicals that inhibit the germination and growth of surrounding plants. Forms dense monocultures that are almost impenetrable to wildlife.

📡 How It Spreads in SG

Wind-dispersed seeds in large quantities. Each plant produces thousands of feathery seeds that float long distances. Thrives particularly in disturbed soil after construction or land clearing — making Singapore's constant development a major driver of its spread.

🛡️ How NParks Removes It

  • Cutting before seed set is critical — prevents next generation
  • Herbicide treatment on cut stumps
  • Replanting with fast-growing native pioneer species for competition
  • Community volunteer cutting events on Pulau Ubin
🤯

Wild fact: Siam Weed is highly flammable when dry — it increases fire risk in areas where it dominates. In Singapore's generally fire-free forests, even a small fire from a discarded cigarette in a Siam Weed patch could be devastating.

HIGH

Albizia

Falcataria moluccana

"The Widow-Maker Tree"

📍 Maluku, Indonesia🌍 Found: Parks, forests📏 Grows 7m/year

🏞️ Habitat in Singapore

Extremely common across Singapore in secondary forests, parks, and roadside plantings. So fast-growing it was deliberately planted for shade — before its invasive and dangerous nature was understood. Now found throughout Pulau Ubin and Central Catchment.

💀 How It Kills

One of the world's fastest-growing trees — up to 7 metres per year. This rapid growth produces structurally weak wood that breaks catastrophically in Singapore's frequent storms, creating dangerous widow-maker falls. Its dense canopy shades out native forest regeneration completely.

📡 How It Spreads in SG

Wind and water-dispersed seeds produced in enormous quantities. Its nitrogen-fixing roots give it an advantage in poor soils, allowing it to outcompete native trees that need better soil conditions to establish.

🛡️ How NParks Removes It

  • Ring-barking for large specimens — kills tree slowly to prevent sudden collapse
  • Controlled felling by arborists — dangerous due to structural weakness
  • Stump treatment with herbicide to prevent regrowth
  • Pulau Ubin Albizia removal is a major ongoing NParks project
🤯

Wild fact: The Albizia was planted so extensively in Singapore for reforestation in the 1970s that it now dominates large areas of secondary forest — a well-intentioned decision that created a major ecological problem.

MEDIUM

Lantana

Lantana camara

"The Pretty Killer"

📍 C. America🌍 Found: Gardens, edges⚠️ Toxic berries

🏞️ Habitat in Singapore

Roadsides, garden escapes, forest edges, and Pulau Ubin wasteland. Often sold in Singapore plant nurseries as an ornamental — making it one of the few invasives still being actively spread by consumers.

💀 How It Kills

Forms dense prickly thickets that exclude native plants and make areas impenetrable to wildlife. Its berries are poisonous to children and most native mammals (though birds eat them). It also releases allelopathic chemicals that suppress surrounding plant growth.

📡 How It Spreads in SG

Bird-dispersed seeds — particularly by the Javan Myna and Asian Glossy Starling — are the primary spread mechanism in Singapore. Also escapes from gardens that border nature areas.

🛡️ How NParks Removes It

  • Manual removal with thick gloves (thorny stems)
  • Herbicide on cut stumps
  • Campaigns to discourage purchase of Lantana from nurseries
  • Biological control: Lantana has 36+ studied biocontrol agents globally
🤯

Wild fact: Lantana flowers change colour as they age — from yellow to orange to red. This is a signal to pollinators: yellow flowers have nectar, red ones don't. Despite this clever trick, it's a serious invasive across tropical Singapore.

MEDIUM

Giant Salvinia

Salvinia molesta

"The Aquarium Escapee"

📍 S. America🌍 Found: Ponds, canals📏 Doubles in 2.5 days

🏞️ Habitat in Singapore

Found in ornamental ponds, slow-moving canals, and reservoir margins across Singapore. Frequently introduced through the aquarium trade — people buy it as an aquarium plant then dump it in waterways when they no longer want it.

💀 How It Kills

Can double its biomass in as little as 2.5 days under optimal conditions. Creates thick floating mats that block all light, deplete oxygen, and prevent gas exchange at the water surface. Completely eliminates aquatic biodiversity beneath the mat within weeks.

📡 How It Spreads in SG

Almost entirely through human activity — aquarium plant releases are the main vector in Singapore. Fragments also travel through Singapore's canal network during heavy rain. The plant reproduces vegetatively, so even tiny fragments become new plants.

🛡️ How PUB & NParks Remove It

  • Manual skimming from ponds and canals
  • Biological control: Cyrtobagous salviniae weevil (very effective globally)
  • Public awareness campaigns targeting aquarium hobbyists
  • Regulation of aquatic plant sales in Singapore
🤯

Wild fact: Giant Salvinia is covered in tiny water-repelling hairs shaped like miniature egg-beaters — a remarkable natural waterproofing system that helps it float. Scientists have studied these hairs to develop new waterproof materials.

FIELD IDENTIFICATION

How to Spot an Invasive Plant in Singapore 🔎

1

It's at a forest edge or disturbed area

Most Singapore invasives establish first in disturbed soil — construction edges, roadsides, and gaps in vegetation. If you see a single species dominating these areas, be suspicious.

2

It's forming a monoculture

Healthy Singapore forest has extraordinary plant diversity. If you see a large area dominated by just one species — especially a climbing vine or dense shrub — it may be invasive.

3

It's climbing or smothering other plants

Look for vines draping over shrubs and trees like a blanket — a classic sign of Zanzibar Yam or Mile-a-Minute. The plants underneath may already be dying.

4

Use iNaturalist Singapore

Open iNaturalist, photograph the plant, and submit. The Singapore NParks community will help identify it within hours. Your observation becomes scientific data!

⚠️

Important: Never remove invasive plants yourself without NParks guidance. Some (like Albizia) are structurally dangerous. Always report first and let the experts manage removal.

HOW TO REPORT

Step-by-Step: Report an Invasive Plant in Singapore

📱

Step 1: Download iNaturalist

Free on iOS and Android. Singapore has an active community — your observations get responses within hours, often from NParks or NUS researchers.

📸

Step 2: Photograph Clearly

Take multiple photos: the whole plant, leaves (top and bottom), stem, flowers or fruits if visible. The more detail, the more accurate the identification.

📍

Step 3: Enable GPS Location

Make sure location services are on when you photograph — precise GPS coordinates are what make citizen science data useful to researchers.

✅

Step 4: Submit the Observation

Upload to iNaturalist. Add any notes about the infestation size, how dense it is, and whether it's inside or at the edge of forest. All details help.

📢

Step 5: Report Formally if Needed

For serious infestations, contact NParks directly at 1800-471-7300 or submit via the OneService app. For aquatic invasives in canals, contact PUB at 1800-284-66
