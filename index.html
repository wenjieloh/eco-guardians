🌍

Not From Here

Invasive plants are species introduced to places outside their native home.

⚡

💀

Once established, invasive plants are extremely difficult and expensive to get rid of.

What Makes a Plant "Invasive"?

An invasive plant is a species that is non-native to an ecosystem and whose introduction causes harm to the environment, economy, or human health.


A plant becomes invasive when it:

  • Establishes itself in a new environment
  • Reproduces rapidly and spreads aggressively
  • Outcompetes native species for resources
  • Causes ecological or economic damage

Native vs Invasive

🌸 Native Plant
  • Evolved here
  • Has natural enemies
  • Supports local wildlife
  • Grows in balance
  • Part of the food web
🌿 Invasive Plant
  • Brought from elsewhere
  • No natural enemies
  • Pushes wildlife out
  • Takes over fast
  • Disrupts food web
🧬

Why No Enemies?

In their home country, insects, fungi, and animals evolved to control these plants. In a new country, none of these natural controls exist!

⏱️

Explosive Growth

Some invasive plants double their coverage in a single year. Kudzu can grow 30cm in a single day!

🚢

Accidental vs Intentional

Many invasive plants were brought deliberately as garden plants, only to escape and wreak havoc. Others hitched rides as seeds in soil or packaging.

How Do Invasive Plants Spread?

Invasive plants are incredibly clever at moving around. Here are the main ways they spread:

💧

Water

Seeds and plant fragments float downstream, colonising riverbanks and wetlands.

💨

Wind

Lightweight seeds with feathery attachments travel huge distances on the wind.

🐦

Animals & Birds

Animals eat berries and deposit seeds far away. Seeds also stick to fur and feathers.

👟

Humans

We carry seeds on shoes, tyres, and clothes. Dumped garden waste is one of the biggest causes.

🚗

Vehicles

Construction equipment moves soil containing seeds and roots from site to site.

🌱

Underground Roots

Many invasives spread through rhizomes. Cut one plant and the roots sprout five more!

Where Do They Take Over?

Invasive plants can invade almost any habitat:

🌊

Rivers & Wetlands

Water hyacinth and purple loosestrife choke waterways, blocking sunlight and depleting oxygen for fish.

⚠️ Water Hyacinth, Purple Loosestrife
🌲

Forests

Climbing vines smother trees, blocking sunlight. Some release toxins that kill other plants.

⚠️ Kudzu, English Ivy
🌾

Grasslands

Dense grass invaders crowd out wildflowers and the insects that depend on them.

⚠️ Cogon Grass, Cheatgrass
🏙️

Urban Areas

Invasives thrive in disturbed soil along roads, railways, and construction sites.

⚠️ Japanese Knotweed, Buddleja
🌊

Coastal Areas

Salt-tolerant invasives can destroy coastal dunes and mangroves protecting shorelines.

⚠️ Spartina, Ice Plant
🏔️

Islands

Islands are most vulnerable — species evolved in isolation with no defences against invasives.

⚠️ Miconia, Strawberry Guava

The Damage They Cause 💀

Invasive plants are the second biggest cause of species extinction worldwide:

🌿 Environmental Damage

  • Pushes out native plants — competing for light, water, and nutrients
  • Destroys animal habitats — many animals only eat specific native plants
  • Changes soil chemistry — some invasives release toxins that poison the soil
  • Increases wildfire risk — invasive grasses burn faster and hotter
  • Blocks waterways — aquatic invasives choke rivers, reducing oxygen for fish

💰 Economic Damage

  • Reduces crop yields — invasives compete with food crops
  • Costs billions in control — the US alone spends $34 billion/year
  • Damages property — Japanese knotweed can crack foundations and walls
  • Harms fishing industries — aquatic invasives destroy fish habitats

🏥 Human Health Damage

  • Allergens — invasive plants often produce more pollen than natives
  • Toxic plants — some (like Lantana) are poisonous to children and pets
  • Tick habitats — dense undergrowth creates homes for ticks carrying Lyme disease
  • Water quality — algal blooms contaminate drinking water

How Do We Fight Back? 🛡️

Several methods exist for controlling invasive plants — each with pros and cons:

✋

Manual Removal

Best for small areas

Hand-pulling, digging, or cutting. Pros: No chemicals, targeted. Cons: Labour-intensive, must remove all roots.

🧪

Herbicides

Use carefully

Chemical sprays. Pros: Effective on large areas. Cons: Can harm native plants, enters waterways.

🐛

Biological Control

Long-term solution

Introducing natural enemies. Pros: Sustainable. Cons: Takes years, must be thoroughly tested first.

🌱

Replanting Natives

Essential step

After removing invasives, plant native species. Pros: Restores ecosystem. Cons: Slow, ongoing effort.

🚫

Prevention

Most effective!

Stopping new invasives from entering. Includes biosecurity checks, regulations on plant imports, and public awareness. Prevention is always cheaper than cure!

💡

Key tip: Never dump garden waste in natural areas! Many invasions started with a single plant thrown over a fence.

Kudzu

Pueraria montana

"The Vine That Ate the South"

📍 East Asia🌍 USA, Australia📏 30cm/day

🏞️ Habitat

Roadsides, forest edges, disturbed areas. Thrives in warm, humid climates. Spreading northwards with climate change.

💀 How It Kills

Grows over everything — trees, shrubs, other plants. Dense mats block all sunlight and can bring down entire trees through sheer weight.

📡 How It Spreads

Seeds eaten and dispersed by animals. Also spreads through underground rhizomes and stem nodes that root wherever they touch ground.

🛡️ How to Remove

  • Repeated cutting during growing season — exhausts roots
  • Herbicide treatment on cut stems in late summer
  • Grazing with goats — they love eating kudzu!
🤯

Wild fact: Kudzu was sold at garden centres in the 1930s as an ornamental plant! The US government even paid farmers to plant it for erosion control.

💜
HIGH

Water Hyacinth

Eichhornia crassipes

"World's Worst Aquatic Weed"

📍 S. America🌍 Africa, Asia📏 Doubles in 2 weeks

🏞️ Habitat

Lakes, ponds, rivers, and slow-moving waterways. Prefers warm, nutrient-rich water. Devastating in tropical and subtropical regions.

💀 How It Kills

Forms thick floating mats that block sunlight — aquatic plants die and fish suffocate as oxygen drops. Also traps fishing nets and breeds disease-carrying mosquitoes.

📡 How It Spreads

Doubles coverage in just 2 weeks through vegetative reproduction. Floods move large mats to new water bodies.

🛡️ How to Remove

  • Mechanical harvesting with specialist boats
  • Biological control: Neochetina weevils (highly effective!)
  • Composting — removed plants make great fertiliser
🤯

Wild fact: Water hyacinth produces beautiful purple flowers — which is why it was spread as a decorative pond plant. Lake Victoria in Africa lost 90% of its biodiversity after it took over.

🎋
EXTREME

Japanese Knotweed

Reynoutria japonica

"The Concrete Destroyer"

📍 East Asia🌍 Europe, USA, NZ📏 Roots: 3m deep

🏞️ Habitat

Roadsides, riverbanks, waste ground, gardens. Incredibly hardy — grows through tarmac and concrete. Survives drought, flooding, and even fire.

💀 How It Kills

Dense stands block all light. Rhizome network extends 7m sideways and 3m deep — cracking building foundations, walls, and pavements.

📡 How It Spreads

Almost entirely through root fragments — a piece the size of a fingernail can grow a whole new plant. Human activity like digging is the main spread mechanism.

🛡️ How to Remove

  • Specialist herbicide — takes 3–5 years of treatment
  • Excavation and disposal at licensed waste sites (NEVER compost)
  • Biological control: Aphalara itadori psyllid
🤯

Wild fact: In the UK, Japanese knotweed in your garden can prevent you from getting a mortgage on your house. It must be declared when selling property!

🌸
MEDIUM

Lantana

Lantana camara

"The Pretty Killer"

📍 C. America🌍 Africa, Asia⚠️ Toxic to livestock

🏞️ Habitat

Open forests, grasslands, roadsides in tropical and subtropical regions. Common in India, Australia, and parts of Africa.

💀 How It Kills

Forms dense thickets blocking native plant growth. Berries are toxic to humans and most livestock, though birds eat and spread them everywhere.

📡 How It Spreads

Bird-dispersed seeds are the main vector. Also spreads vegetatively. Still sold in garden centres in some countries!

🛡️ How to Remove

  • Manual removal with gloves (skin irritant)
  • Herbicide on cut stumps
  • Biological control: 36 agents tested worldwide
🤯

Wild fact: Lantana's flowers change colour as they age — from yellow to orange to red — to signal to pollinators they've already been visited!

🌾
HIGH

Cogon Grass

Imperata cylindrica

"The Fire Grass"

📍 SE Asia🌍 USA, Africa🔥 Increases fire risk

🏞️ Habitat

Disturbed areas, roadsides, pine forests. Highly adaptable — tolerates poor soils, drought, fire, and heavy grazing.

💀 How It Kills

Releases allelopathic chemicals poisoning the soil. Burns hotter than native grass fires, killing tree seedlings and creating a dangerous fire-cycle feedback loop.

📡 How It Spreads

Wind-dispersed feathery seeds travel miles. Also spreads through rhizomes and regrows denser after fire.

🛡️ How to Remove

  • Systemic herbicide treatment (imazapyr or glyphosate)
  • Repeated mowing to exhaust root reserves
  • Shading with fast-growing native trees
🤯

Wild fact: The Japanese military used cogon grass for thatching during WWII, accidentally spreading it through the Pacific — one of history's most damaging accidental introductions!

🌺
HIGH

Purple Loosestrife

Lythrum salicaria

"The Wetland Destroyer"

📍 Europe & Asia🌍 N. America🌸 2.7M seeds/plant

🏞️ Habitat

Wetlands, marshes, riverbanks, and lake edges. Has invaded approximately 50% of all North American wetlands.

💀 How It Kills

Forms monocultures replacing diverse native wetland plants. The entire food web collapses — waterfowl, amphibians, and insects all decline dramatically.

📡 How It Spreads

Each plant produces up to 2.7 million seeds per year, dispersed by water, wind, and animals' feet. Also spreads through root fragments.

🛡️ How to Remove

  • Biological control using Galerucella beetles — most successful method!
  • Hand-pulling when young (before seeds form)
  • Cutting before flowering to prevent seeding
🤯

Wild fact: Purple loosestrife has three different flower types — each needing pollen from the other two to set seed. Despite this, it still produces millions of seeds yearly!

IDENTIFICATION

How to Spot an Invasive Plant 🔎

1

Growing somewhere unusual

Invasives often appear in disturbed areas — roadsides, building sites, river edges, or recently cleared land.

2

Lots of it — and nothing else

Invasives often form monocultures. Natural areas have many different plant species mixed together.

3

Growing over everything

Climbing or sprawling plants covering other vegetation is a classic sign of an invasive species.

4

Use iNaturalist

Download the free iNaturalist app, photograph the plant, and the community will help you identify it!

⚠️

Important: Never touch or remove plants you don't recognise! Always tell an adult and report through official channels.

STEP BY STEP

How to Report an Invasive Plant

📱

Step 1: Download iNaturalist

Get the free app (iOS or Android). Used by scientists and conservationists worldwide.

📸

Step 2: Photograph the Plant

Take clear photos of the leaves, stem, flowers, and surroundings. Don't touch unknown plants.

🔍

Step 3: Submit Your Observation

Upload to iNaturalist. The community will help identify the plant with your GPS location.

📢

Step 4: Report to Authorities

Contact your local council or conservation department to report confirmed sightings.

🌱

Step 5: Follow Up

Check if action was taken. Contact a local conservation group if nothing happens.

SAFETY FIRST

What NOT to Do ⚠️

🚫

Don't touch unknown plants

Some invasives (like Giant Hogweed) can cause severe skin burns. Always identify first and get adult supervision.

🚫

Don't pull and leave them

Dropped root fragments and seeds can just grow new plants. Bag and dispose of correctly.

🚫

Don't compost invasive plants

Most survive home composting and re-establish when compost is used. Take to a certified green waste facility.

🚫

Don't tackle large areas alone

Large-scale removal requires specialists. Contact local conservation groups or your council.
